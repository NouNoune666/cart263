Project One Response

https://philippe-bn.github.io/cart263/projects/project-1/

This is Phil's project. I thoroughly enjoyed it and spent many minutes watching the little fish follow my cursor. It is an effective and straightforward concept: a group of fish in an aquarium move together toward you. However, once you look into the code, there are layers to it that make the whole experience much more enjoyable. The fish don't simply move toward the cursor, they do so in a specific, deliberate way. They never overlap each other; when they get too close, they veer off in different directions. Another subtle detail is the handling of speed, or rather force. If you stop moving the mouse, the fish drift off and do their own thing. Once you move again, they accelerate back toward the cursor. Looking through the code, I can roughly understand how Phil achieves this, and I find that attention to detail genuinely inspiring. Then there's the aesthetic: beautiful colors that pop, simple fish forms that are immediately readable. It's also a quiet metaphor for being trapped. The beauty of the aquarium doesn't mean you aren't confined, it's something of an illusion.

https://norahwilcox.github.io/CART263/projects/project-1/Project1/

This is Nora's project, and it immediately stood out to me for its aesthetic. The whole website is styled after early 2000s Windows: a pixellised background image of the classic mountain, the oldschool interface, the buttons, etc.. All of it is executed with consistency. What impressed me most technically is how much ground the project covers. Nora’s team used live video capture, which we hadn't studied at that point in the course, and the photos taken actually persist within the website rather than disappearing. Beyond this, the CSS alone is remarkable. Every element, from the tab bar to the timer buttons, is carefully considered and on-theme. Reading through it, I can see how much detailed work went into it. On my end, I'm still working with fairly basic CSS and don't yet have a full grasp of all its possibilities. One particularly thoughtful touch is the Safari fallback sectio. It's a problem that went beyond the scope of the class, and the fact that it was  considered says a lot about the care put into this project.

https://emilebedard.github.io/cart263/Projects/project1/Interactive_Bike_Garage/

Last but not least, here is Emile's project. Knowing how passionate he is about cycling, it was genuinely nice to see that come through in the work. The aesthetic is clean, calm, and fresh and I can see he put a lot of consideration into it. I also appreciated learning more about bikes in general through the maintenance section. The information bits of the project are pretty timely considering i’ll be taking out my summer bike soon. Similarly to Nora's project, one thing that stood out to me is that the website remembers what you've submitted. You can add multiple bikes, fill in their parts, and the entries stay there as you navigate. This is done using local storage. Every time you add a bike or register a part, the data is saved there and when the page loads, it reads from that same storage and rebuilds what you entered. It's a relatively simple concept but it makes the experience feel much more like a real tool. It is also something I'd like to try implementing myself.
